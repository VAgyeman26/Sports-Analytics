# Sports-Analytics
This is for my sports Analytics Innovation Assignment
## Project Overview
This project proposes a Player Workload and Injury Risk Index (PWIRI) for use within a professional basketball organization. The PWIRI is a composite metric that aggregates player workload data from multiple sources, including minutes played, distance covered per game, schedule density, and soft-tissue injury history, to generate a real-time risk score for each player on the roster.
Rather than relying on isolated statistics or subjective coaching intuition, the PWIRI synthesizes continuous tracking data into a single interpretable value that sits between 0 and 100, where higher scores indicate elevated injury risk. The tool is intended to shift the organization from reactive injury management toward proactive load management, allowing medical and coaching staff to make evidence-based decisions about rest, rotation, and minutes restrictions before physical breakdowns occur.
## Decision Making
Coaching staffs and front offices in professional basketball regularly face a tension between competitive performance goals and player availability over the course of a long season. Without a systematic framework for monitoring and interpreting workload data, organizations tend to either overplay high-value athletes in short-term situations or rotate players inconsistently in ways that disrupt team rhythm.
The PWIRI addresses a specific decision problem: how should a head coach and performance staff determine which players are at elevated risk of soft-tissue injury during a given game week, and how should that information influence rotation decisions? Currently, many organizations address this informally through conversations between coaches and athletic trainers. The PWIRI formalizes and standardizes this process so that decision-relevant information is visible, comparable across players, and consistently applied throughout the season.
## Proposed Analytics Approach
The index would draw on three primary data sources. The first is optical tracking data, which modern arenas capture through systems such as Second Spectrum or Hawk-Eye, providing per-possession measures of distance covered, acceleration events, and deceleration loads. The second is scheduling data maintained by the league, which captures back-to-back games, travel distance between cities, and days of rest between contests. The third is longitudinal player health records maintained by team medical staff, including documented soft-tissue injuries and return-to-play timelines.
Using these inputs, a weighted composite score would be calculated for each player on a rolling seven-day basis. The weighting scheme would be informed by existing research in sports science on the relationship between acute workload ratios and injury probability. Linear regression would be used initially to establish coefficients, with the model evaluated against historical injury occurrences in the organization's internal records. The output is not a prediction in the strict causal sense but a risk-stratification tool that flags players who warrant closer attention from the performance staff.
## Use by Decison Makers
The primary users of the PWIRI would be the head coach, assistant coaches responsible for player development, and the head of performance science. On a game-by-game basis, the tool would surface in a simple dashboard view showing each player's current risk score alongside their workload trend over the preceding two weeks. Color-coded indicators would allow coaching staff to immediately identify high-risk players without needing to interpret underlying data.
In practical terms, the head coach might use the dashboard during morning shootaround to decide whether a player who logged heavy minutes over the previous three games should be restricted in practice or given a reduced role in an upcoming contest. The performance science staff would use the same data to have structured conversations with medical personnel about proactive interventions. Because the tool generates a single interpretable number rather than a complex model output, it is designed to fit naturally into the brief pre-game and pre-practice windows that coaching staff realistically have for analytics review.

## Prototype Enhancement
The original PWIRI calculates an individual risk score for each player in isolation. This prototype enhancement proposes extending the model to incorporate a team-level fatigue clustering dimension, which would allow the tool to flag not only individual injury risk but also collective periods where multiple players are simultaneously in elevated-risk states.
What is being changed: The enhancement adds a second analytical layer to the existing individual scoring framework. Using k-means clustering on the seven-day rolling workload vectors of all active roster players, the model would identify periods when a disproportionate share of the roster is carrying high fatigue loads simultaneously. This cluster state would be reported alongside individual scores as a contextual indicator, allowing coaching staff to understand whether a high individual risk score is an isolated finding or part of a broader team-wide fatigue pattern.
Why this change could improve decision-making: Individual risk scores can be misleading when assessed without context. A player with a moderately elevated score during a period when the rest of the roster is well-rested represents a different situation than the same score during a stretch when most of the rotation is fatigued. Coaching decisions about rest and rotation are inherently collective, involving tradeoffs between multiple players rather than one individual at a time. By surfacing team-level fatigue states alongside individual scores, the tool becomes more directly useful for the kinds of decisions coaches actually face, particularly during compressed stretches of the schedule where rest management is most consequential
## Reflection on Innovation and Version Control
How branches support low-risk experimentation
One of the persistent challenges in analytics organizations is that promising ideas often fail not because they are technically flawed but because the act of testing them disrupts existing workflows or creates confusion among stakeholders who encounter unfinished work. Branching in GitHub addresses this directly by creating isolated environments where new ideas can be developed, documented, and refined without any risk of contaminating the stable version of a project.
In this assignment, the prototype branch allowed the team-level fatigue clustering enhancement to be fully articulated and examined before any decision was made about whether it belonged in the main project. Coaching staff and decision makers interacting with the main branch during that period would have seen only the polished original concept, not a work in progress. This mirrors how analytics teams in professional organizations manage the tension between experimentation and reliability. A data scientist can iterate aggressively on a new model version without exposing the front office to incomplete outputs that might undermine confidence in the analytics function as a whole.

How GitHub helps analytics ideas gain traction with decision makers
A common failure mode in sports analytics is that technically sound ideas never achieve organizational adoption because they are communicated through informal conversations, one-off spreadsheets, or presentations that have no persistent reference point. GitHub addresses this by creating a durable, version-controlled record of how an analytics idea has evolved, what alternatives were considered, and why specific design decisions were made.
For decision makers who are not deeply technical, the commit history and branch structure of a GitHub repository serve as a transparency mechanism. They can see that the analytics team tested multiple approaches, evaluated each one against explicit criteria, and made a reasoned integration decision rather than simply presenting a finished product whose development history is invisible. This visibility builds trust over time. It also makes it easier to revisit and explain past decisions when the organization faces new situations that resemble earlier problems, creating an institutional memory that informal analytics workflows rarely produce.

Alignment with the Chapter 7 innovation framework

The README content developed in Q1 represents the creative phase: a clearly articulated analytics idea with a defined problem, proposed method, and intended user. It is a practical process that can be operationalized through tools like GitHub, turning what might otherwise be an informal and invisible creative process into a documented, auditable, and reproducible workflow.
